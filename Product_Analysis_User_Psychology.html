Pure necessity
🧊
Refrigerator
Practical · wife-led · brand loyalty high
What it means
The refrigerator carries no emotional aspiration. It is a kitchen necessity. The decision is entirely practical — capacity, fit, brand familiarity, and energy cost. Brand loyalty is generational: if her mother had Whirlpool, she likely wants Whirlpool. Samsung's 2023 India-manufactured fridge range launch was specifically designed for Indian preferences — a signal that this category is localising fast.
Decision pattern
Research time: 1–2 weeks (shortest of all categories)
Who decides: Wife decides completely — capacity, layout (single/double door), colour. Husband approves price only.
Trigger: Summer heat ("food is spoiling faster"), new home, old fridge failure
Marriage gifting: High — fridges are among the most common marriage gifts in Jaipur's middle class

Recommending a fridge without asking the kitchen dimensions. The customer returns it or leaves angry. Always ask: "Aapke kitchen mein jagah kitni hai?" first.

Footfall driver
💨
Fan
Pure utility · fastest purchase cycle
What it means
A commodity. The customer is not excited to buy a fan — they need one and want the transaction done quickly. However, fans are your highest-volume category (3.65L units in Jaipur annually) and a critical footfall driver. A customer who comes in for a ₹1,500 fan can be asked about their AC needs. Never treat fan buyers as small sales — they are the first conversation.
Decision pattern
Research time: Under 30 minutes
Who decides: Whoever is home — individual, impulsive, low deliberation
Trigger: Old fan breaks, new room being set up, pre-summer, new house
Emerging segment: BLDC (energy-efficient) fans at ₹3,500–5,000 are growing fast among upper-middle buyers. Stock 2–3 BLDC models.

The upsell moment
Fan buyer → ask about AC ("Ghar mein AC hai?"). A simple question converts a ₹1,500 transaction into a lead for a ₹40,000 sale. Train every staff member to ask this.

Behavioural science

The psychology running every purchase decision

These are not tactics — they are how human brains make decisions under uncertainty. Every store layout, pricing display, and staff script should be designed around at least one of these principles. Each is grounded in established behavioural economics research.

Anchoring
The first price seen becomes the reference point for all subsequent judgements. Show the ₹58,000 model before the ₹38,000 model. The second feels like a bargain even if it was always the plan.
→ Show premium model first in every zone. PLP: list higher-priced models at top.
Kahneman & Tversky, Judgement Under Uncertainty (1974) · Thaler, Mental Accounting Matters (1999)
Social proof
People follow crowd behaviour when uncertain. "Ye model is season mein sabse zyada bika" eliminates decision paralysis — particularly powerful in Tier 2 where peer networks are tight.
→ Bestseller badge on 1 product per category only. Staff verbal script: confirm the social proof claim.
Cialdini, Influence (1984) · BCG-Google 2019 confirms peer influence high in Tier 2
Endowment effect
Once someone physically handles a product, they begin to feel psychological ownership. Sunita pressing the WM buttons is not demo — it is ownership creation. Touch converts.
→ Design every product zone for physical interaction. Especially critical for WM and fridge.
Thaler, Toward a Positive Theory of Consumer Choice (1980) · Kahneman et al., Anomalies (1991)
Mental accounting
₹1,780/month and ₹31,990 are psychologically processed in different mental "accounts." The monthly amount feels manageable even when the total is identical. This is why EMI-first framing works.
→ Always display EMI/month as the primary price across all channels and all categories.
Thaler, Mental Accounting Matters (1999) · GRIN EMI study 2024 confirms for Indian CE market
Peak-end rule
Memory of an experience is shaped by its most intense moment and its final moment — not the average. The billing moment and the goodbye are what Ramesh will describe to his neighbour next week.
→ Fast billing (<5 min). Warm, named goodbye. Day +2 WhatsApp. These are your marketing.
Kahneman, Fredrickson et al., When More Pain is Preferred to Less (1993)
Choice overload
More options systematically reduce purchase rates. Showing 12 AC models paralyses Sunita. Three curated options (value / recommended / premium) make the decision manageable.
→ Max 3 options per category in the PLP and in-store. Your job is to edit, not display everything.
Iyengar & Lepper, When Choice is Demotivating (2000) · Confirmed in Indian retail context
Authority bias
Customers trust demonstrated expertise over salesmanship. "1.5T kaafi hai, 2T waste hoga aapke room ke liye" positions staff as trusted advisors, not commission-hunters.
→ Train staff to lead with expertise, not enthusiasm. The advisor beats the salesperson every time.
Milgram (1963) · Cialdini (1984) · Validated in Indian high-value retail interactions
Loss aversion
People feel losses ~2× more acutely than equivalent gains. "Ye offer kal khatam hai" works because missing out hurts more than the discount feels good. Use sparingly — overuse kills trust.
→ One genuine scarcity signal per visit. Festive season is the right time. Year-round use destroys credibility.
Kahneman & Tversky, Prospect Theory (1979)
ROBO behaviour
Research Online, Buy Offline. Tier 2 customers increasingly research on digital platforms but complete the purchase in-store. BCG-Google (2019) found Tier 2 digital influence grew 5× — but the store remains where money is spent.
→ Your online presence (GMB, WhatsApp catalogue) exists to bring people in — not to compete with Amazon.
BCG-Google Digital Influence 2019 · Amoghvarta Tier 2 study 2024

Where customers drop — and why
Awareness → Visit: GMB rating below 4.2 or no photos. Fix: collect reviews from Day 1.
Visit → Engage: Staff on phones or hovering too early. Fix: 90-second breathing room rule.
Consider → Decision: Amazon price objection without a ready answer. Fix: arm every staff member with the local service script.
Decision → Buy: Slow billing or pushy second upsell. Fix: <5 min billing, one upsell only.
Buy → Refer: Not asking. Fix: Day +2 WhatsApp with review link and referral ask. Nobody asks — you will.
The one insight that changes everything
Indian consumers — even in Tier 2 cities — now walk into your store with Amazon open on their phones. They already know the online price. The question they are really asking is: "Give me a reason to buy from you instead."

Your answer is: local installation, local service, EMI without a credit card, and someone who speaks Hindi and knows their name.
